Promises vs. Reality

A chronology of the 179th Street corridor — from the first infrastructure promises through today. Every entry is sourced from public meeting minutes, County documents, or official records.

Promise / commitment
Warning / concern raised
Development approved
Deadline missed / failure documented
2004–2007: The Setup
2004 & 2007
Area brought into Vancouver’s Urban Growth Area
Approximately 2,200 acres surrounding the 179th Street corridor are added to the UGA. Urban holding overlay applied immediately because transportation infrastructure lacks adequate capacity for urban development.
2014: The Rules Change
August 2014
County changes how concurrency is measured
Ordinance 2014-08-09 shifts LOS methodology from travel speed standards to volume-to-capacity ratios. The entire travel speed table (38 corridor segments) is deleted.
“When we made this change, it dramatically lowered our needed projects and subsequently lowered our TIF rates as a result.” — Matt Herman, Public Works, March 2019 Council Work Session
2015–2016: State Funding
2015–2016
Connecting Washington package funds I-5/179th interchange
The state legislature allocates $50 million for the I-5/179th interchange replacement, with funds scheduled for the 2023–2025 biennium. This becomes the centerpiece of the corridor improvement plan.
2018: Warnings Ignored
November 7, 2018
WSDOT recommends denial of urban holding lift
In a letter to Community Planning Director Oliver Orjiako, WSDOT states the existing interchange lacks capacity for additional development and won’t be operationally functional until 2028. WSDOT supports the staff recommendation of denial.
“WSDOT supports the staff report recommendation of denial for this proposal.” — WSDOT letter to Oliver Orjiako, November 7, 2018
Never disclosed at any subsequent hearing
December 18, 2018
Phase 1: Urban holding lifted — Killian Pacific
40 acres approved for 200 single-family homes and a 326-unit apartment complex. Council approves without knowledge of the WSDOT denial recommendation issued 41 days earlier.
2019: The Floodgates Open
February 21, 2019
Phase 2: Planning Commission approves Holt Homes lift
143 acres approved for 606 single-family homes and 99 townhomes. Commissioner Bender asks if there’s a construction plan. Public Works describes a “goal” — no actual plan exists.
“I just wanted to make sure that we weren’t misleading you, that there isn’t a true construction management plan sitting there.” — Randy Printz, developers’ representative, Feb 21, 2019
No construction plan. Approved 4-0.
February–March 2019
179th Street Council Subcommittee meets
Goal: bring corridor into concurrency compliance. Reasonably fund projects in the six-year TIP. Funding options debated; bonding discussed then punted to the working group and Council.
July 16, 2019
Council approves Phase 2 urban holding lift
WSDOT mentioned only in passing. Ahmad Qayoumi notes the interchange is “funded through state” and they’re meeting monthly. No mention of WSDOT’s denial recommendation or capacity concerns.
August 20, 2019
Council adopts $66.5 million funding plan
Resolution 2019-08-05 selects financing plan for Step 1 corridor projects. Funding sources include road funds, REET, grants, developer TIF payments, and surcharges. Step 2 projects (NE 15th–26th and NE 29th–50th widening) are not funded.
September 10, 2019
Open House at Alki Middle School
County presents project timeline showing major corridor improvements completing between 2021 and 2027.
Original timeline: 2021–2027
October 1, 2019
Phase 3: Hinton Development — 32 acres, 129 homes
Planning Commission approves 4-1. Commissioner Barca casts the sole dissenting vote, warning the County is “running out of capacity long before we will have done the build-out for this area.”
“So the developments may be finished before the things are in place?” “Correct.” — Public testimony question and Matt Hermen’s response, October 8, 2019
November 12, 2019
Full urban holding removal: all 2,200 acres
County Council lifts urban holding on the remaining acreage. The six-year GMA clock starts. All corridor improvements must be in place or financially committed by November 2025.
Six-year deadline: November 2025
2020–2022: Development Proceeds
2020
WSU Vancouver master plan updated
Approximately 1,300 trips reserved under a concurrency agreement for future campus expansion.
2021
179th Street Access Management & Circulation Plan initiated
Planning process begins for managing access and circulation along the corridor as development accelerates.
2023: The Admission
July 12, 2023
Public Works Director admits the timeline was never feasible
Ken Lader tells the County Council that no one with experience believed the six-year timeline was possible when it was first proposed.
“When I first saw these corridor projects back when it was talked about as a package and my predecessors were discussing completing these in six years, I don’t know of anyone who had done projects like these that thought that was possible.” — Ken Lader, Public Works Director, July 12, 2023
July 18, 2023
Arterial Atlas amendments adopted
County Council adopts Planning Commission recommendation (6-0) to implement the 179th Street Access Management and Circulation Plan through Arterial Atlas amendments.
2025: The Deadline Passes
February–June 2025
Five development applications filed with traffic studies
Kozy Manor Estates (14 lots), Four Creeks North (39 lots), Three Creeks East (200 lots), Towhee Creek PUD (96 lots), and Anderson Dental all file traffic impact studies. Five of these studies are prepared during the same period and none count traffic from the other four.
November 2025
Six-year deadline passes
Not a single major corridor project is complete. The deadline established when urban holding was lifted in November 2019 has expired. Infrastructure completion dates now extend to 2035 and beyond.
DEADLINE MISSED — No major projects complete
October 2025
Draft Environmental Impact Statement published
The DEIS for the 2025 Comprehensive Plan update is released. The County’s own travel demand model shows corridor failures, but the DEIS does not identify them as concurrency deficiencies.
December 2025
NE 174th Street Subdivision files traffic study
101-lot subdivision. The traffic study shows the corridor failing by 2028 under background conditions — even assuming planned improvements are built. Prepared by the same engineer as four other corridor studies.
2026: Approvals Continue
January–March 2026
Three corridor projects approved by Hearing Examiner
Four Creeks North (January 21), Kozy Manor Estates (February 27), and Three Creeks East (March 6) are all approved. Combined: 253 lots added to the corridor. All rely on the same unbuilt TIP projects.
March 10, 2026
Hergesheimer v. Clark County decided
Superior Court affirms Hearing Examiner approval of Mill Creek Meadows (40-lot subdivision), applying substantial evidence deference to staff’s interpretation of capacity standards.
March 26, 2026
Meeting with Public Works staff
Corridor residents meet with Jeremy Provenzano (County Engineer), Steve Gallup, David Jardin, and Jose Alvarez. David Jardin confirms the intersection override practice on the record. Jeremy Provenzano characterizes County projects as “mostly independent” of the WSDOT interchange — contradicting prior public statements by Ken Lader and other staff.
What’s Ahead
April 27, 2026
County Council votes on Comprehensive Plan preferred alternative
The decision that sets density and infrastructure commitments for the next 20 years. The corridor is already failing under current approved development.
YOUR VOICE MATTERS
2025–2027 (projected)
NE 179th at NE 29th Ave Roundabout
$27.6 million. The first major corridor project. 2 years past the original deadline.
2028–2030 (projected)
NE 179th (15th–26th Ave) and NE 15th Ave widening
$44.3 million combined. 3–5 years past deadline.
2029–2031 (projected)
I-5/179th Interchange reconstruction (WSDOT)
$86 million. A state project the County cannot build or guarantee. Has slipped repeatedly from its original schedule. 4–6 years past deadline.
2031–2033 (projected)
NE 179th (NE 29th–NE 50th Ave)
$23.9 million. Not on the County’s “Reasonably Funded Project List.” The segment most frequently cited to justify development approvals at the corridor’s eastern end. 6–8 years past deadline.
NOT FUNDED
2035+ (projected)
NE 50th Ave Roundabout
~$24 million estimated. Not funded. Not scheduled. The intersection already shows LOS F in traffic studies. 10+ years past deadline.
NOT FUNDED · NOT SCHEDULED
Unknown
NW 11th/179th Roundabout and Delfel–NW 11th widening
Not in the County’s plans. No timeline. No budget. No funding source identified.
NOT IN ANY PLAN

From the first approvals in 2018 to when the last funded project finishes is nearly 20 years for 2.5 miles of road — and that doesn’t include the west side projects.
